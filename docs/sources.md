Evidence on whether blocked premature atrial contractions constitute atrioventricular block
Overview of the clinical question

Question. Blocked premature atrial contractions (PACs) can produce pauses because a premature atrial beat arrives so early that the atrioventricular (AV) node is still refractory. Does this situation represent a type of AV block or is it simply a premature supraventricular beat that fails to conduct?

ECG interpreters may see a non‑conducted P wave and think of second‑degree AV block. However, highly regarded textbooks, journal articles and guidelines emphasize that blocked PACs are not considered pathologic AV block; they occur because of the normal refractory behavior of the AV node, not because of disease of the conduction system. The evidence below summarizes how blocked PACs differ from AV block and shows why they should not be categorised as AV block.

Thoracic Key – Field Guide to the Arrhythmias	This electrophysiology textbook explains that a P wave that occurs too early to be conducted (“a blocked PAC”) is not heart block. It notes that the early P wave arrives while the AV node is still refractory; therefore “it is too early to be conducted and is not considered heart block at all”. A separate section emphasises that blocked PACs should be treated as ordinary premature atrial complexes and should not be confused with sinus pause or AV block.
ClinicalGate – Disturbances of Rate and Rhythm of the Heart	The pediatric cardiology chapter notes that PACs may be conducted normally, aberrantly or blocked when they occur very early. Non‑conducted PACs occur because the AV node is still refractory, not because of AV block; the resulting pause is non‑compensatory and the P wave may be hidden in the preceding T wave.
ClinicalGate – Atrioventricular Conduction Abnormalities	A figure describes sinus rhythm with atrial trigeminy due to blocked premature atrial complexes mimicking Mobitz type II AV block; the non‑conducted P waves occur early on the T wave and are preceded by normal PR intervals. The chapter stresses that failure of the AV node to conduct during fast atrial tachyarrhythmias due to normal refractoriness should not be considered pathologic AV block; instead, conduction ratios (e.g., 3:2 or 2:1) should be used.
Radiopaedia – Blocked premature atrial contractions	Radiopaedia notes that blocked PACs are a type of bradyarrhythmia. They occur when a premature atrial beat happens so early that it is not conducted through the ventricles because the AV node or His bundle is still refractory.
Radiopaedia – Fetal premature atrial contractions	In the fetal context, Radiopaedia states that non‑conducted PACs can mimic AV block but underscores that AV blocks maintain consistent intervals between atrial events, whereas blocked PACs cause a non‑compensatory pause with irregular atrial intervals.
Evidence from clinical articles and case reports
Source	Evidence
Case report – Symptomatic bradycardia due to blocked PACs (Indian Pacing & Electrophysiology Journal)	A patient presented with bradycardia due to blocked atrial bigeminy. The authors note that non‑conducted P waves may superimpose on T waves and be misdiagnosed as sinoatrial or second‑degree AV block. They emphasise that careful ECG analysis shows that blocked atrial bigeminy has variable P‑P intervals, whereas true second‑degree AV block has constant P‑P intervals, and that misdiagnosis could lead to unnecessary pacemaker implantation.
Blocked atrial bigeminy case report (Dovepress)	This 2025 case report explains that blocked PACs do not necessarily reflect a disorder of the conduction system of the heart. Instead, the coupling interval of a PAC determines whether it will be conducted, aberrantly conducted or blocked. Very early PACs are blocked because the AV node is still refractory. The authors stress that blocked atrial bigeminy should be differentiated from sinus bradycardia, sinus exit block and type 2:1 second‑degree AV block, since those are true bradyarrhythmias that may require pacemaker therapy.
American College of Cardiology – ECG of the Month (2017)	An educational case from the ACC presents an ECG with blocked PACs. The discussion points out that the blocked PACs occur early on the T wave, that the PR interval before and after the blocked beats is unchanged, and that the premature P waves do not “march”. This differentiates them from Mobitz I or Mobitz II AV block, in which PR intervals either progressively lengthen (Mobitz I) or remain constant with regular P‑P intervals (Mobitz II).
Evidence from guidelines and obstetric cardiology
Source	Evidence
Contemporary management of fetal therapy in prenatal cardiology (Polish Society of Gynecologists & Obstetricians, 2025)	This guideline states that bradycardia due to non‑conducted (blocked) supraventricular extrasystoles (atrial bigeminy) is benign and does not require treatment. It distinguishes this arrhythmia from atrioventricular block by noting that in blocked atrial bigeminy the atrial rhythm is irregular and may be faster than the ventricular rhythm, whereas in second‑degree AV block the atrial rhythm is usually regular and the conduction ratio (e.g., 2:1 or 3:1) is constant.
ISUOG VISUOG – Fetal bradycardia	The International Society of Ultrasound in Obstetrics and Gynecology lists multiple blocked atrial ectopic beats and blocked atrial bigeminy as causes of fetal bradycardia separate from AV block. It distinguishes them from immune‑mediated AV block, which occurs in fetuses of mothers with SSA/SSB antibodies and has a different pathogenesis and prognosis.
Key points from the evidence

Mechanism. Blocked PACs occur when a premature atrial impulse arrives while the AV node or His‑Purkinje system is still in its refractory period. Because the conduction system cannot conduct a second impulse so soon, the premature P wave is not followed by a QRS complex. This is not due to disease of the AV node but due to its normal physiologic refractory behaviour.

Not classified as AV block. Standard electrophysiology texts explicitly state that P waves that arrive too early to be conducted are not considered heart block. Blocked PACs are essentially the same arrhythmia as conducted PACs and are categorised as supraventricular ectopic beats rather than atrioventricular block.

Differentiation on ECG. In second‑degree AV block, the P‑P interval remains constant and either the PR interval gradually lengthens (Mobitz I) or remains constant with intermittent dropped beats (Mobitz II). In contrast, blocked PACs produce premature P waves with a coupling interval shorter than the preceding sinus interval; the P waves do not march at a fixed rate. Close inspection often reveals that the blocked P wave is buried in the preceding T wave and the pause that follows is non‑compensatory.

Clinical relevance. Because blocked PACs are benign and do not signify AV nodal disease, they rarely require treatment. Misdiagnosing them as Mobitz II AV block or sinus node dysfunction may lead to unnecessary pacemaker implantation. Case reports and guidelines stress that careful ECG analysis prevents this error.

Conclusion

The weight of evidence from electrophysiology textbooks, peer‑reviewed case reports and clinical guidelines shows that blocked premature atrial contractions are not classified as atrioventricular (AV) block. They occur because a premature atrial impulse encounters a physiologically refractory AV node, not because of structural or functional pathology of the AV node or His–Purkinje system. Although blocked PACs can mimic Mobitz II AV block by producing non‑conducted P waves and pauses, they are distinguished by their premature timing, irregular P‑P intervals and unchanged PR intervals. Recognising blocked PACs as benign supraventricular ectopic beats prevents misdiagnosis and unnecessary interventions.
